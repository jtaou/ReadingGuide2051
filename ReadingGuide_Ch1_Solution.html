•Why are significant figures useful?

They are used to express the precision of a measuring tool used to measure a value

We assume that you already have some familiarity with methods of using significant figures, and that you will be able to apply those rules. However, the rules are not always used in this class. Except on LON-CAPA it is safest to give answers using those rules; it may not always be required though, so it's a good idea to ask if in doubt.

•Our LON-CAPA homework assignments will usually ask you to enter 3 significant figures for your answers. You need to understand how to do this correctly. A number like 0.001 has only 1 significant figure, not 3 or 4 because the leading zeros are not significant. The way to see this is to write it in Scientific Notation (0.001 = 1 x 10^-3 -- there is only 1 significant figure in that). Significant figures arise because our knowledge is limited by what we can measure and in real life, we can only measure quantities to a certain level of precision (that depends on the measuring tools we have). Some physical quantities have been measured to many decimal places, but only by means of exquisitely careful measurement with the absolutely best tools. With the tools you'll have in lab you'll rarely be able to measure quantities to better than 3 significant figures, so giving lots and lots of digits after a decimal place is nonsense from an epistemological point of view.

1.7 Solving Problems in Physics

•What are the three stages recommended in the book for problem solving?

Strategy, Solution, Significance.

•What is the problem solving strategy?

Make a list of what is given or can be inferred from the problem as stated (identify the “knowns”) Identify exactly what needs to be determined in the problem (identify the unknowns). Determine which physical principles can help you solve the problem.

•NOTE: Making a sketch is almost always an essential part of problem solving

•How do you find the numerical solution to a problem?

Substitute the knowns (along with their units) into the appropriate equation and obtain numerical solutions complete with units.

•What do you do to check the significance of the answer?

Check your units. Check the answer to see whether it is reasonable. Check to see whether the answer tells you something interesting.

For the following, we use the LON-CAPA conventions that are also common to many computer programming languages): "^" means exponentiation (e.g. x^2 is x squared), "/" means "divide by", "*" means "multiply by", and "<>" means "not equal to".

•For exponents, you should remember that:

x^(-a) = 1/(x^a)

x^0 = 1 (for x <> 0)

x^(a+b) = (x^a)*(x^b)

x^(1/n) = nth root of x.

•For logarithms, you should remember that:

log(a^b) = b * log(a)

log(ab) = log(a) + log(b)

•Note that log(a + b) <> log(a) + log(b). If you get log(a+b), you are stuck unless you can use an expansion to get an approximate formula.
