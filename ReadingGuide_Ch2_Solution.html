2.1 Scalers and Vectors

  • What is a scalar quantity?
    • Quantity that is fully specified by a single number and unit.
  • What are some examples of scalar quantities?
    • Temperature. Calories.
  • What us a vector quantity?
    • Quantity that is fully specified by a number of units and direction.
  • How are vectors represented geometrically (see Figure 2.2)? Where does it start and end?
    • Geometrically, vectors are represented by arrows, with the tail at the origin and an arrowhead at the end.
  • How are vectors and scalars represented in this book?
    • A vector is a boldface letter with an arrow above it. A scalar is a boldface letter without an arrow.
  • In Figure 2.3, what is the arrow that starts at point A and ends at point B called? What is its’ length called?
    • A displacement vector. The magnitude.
  • Does the displacement vector tell us what the actual path of the particle is?
    • No. Just the distance and direction from the start and end points.
  • What does it mean for two vectors to be antiparallel? Parallel? Orthogonal? See the summary in Figure 2.5.
    • Antiparallel vectors point 180 degrees from each other. Parallel vectors point in the same direction. Orthogonal vectors are perpendicular to one another.
  • What is a vector equation?
    • Equation with vectors on the left and right hand sides.
  • What is a scalar equation?
    • Equation with scalars on the left and right hand sides.
  • Understand Figure 2.7. It shows how one-dimensional vector quantities are added or subtracted.
  • What is a “resultant”?
    • The sum of 2 or more vectors.
  • What does it mean to say that vector addition is commutative and associative?
    • Commutative – A+B=B+A. associative (A+B)+C = A+(B+C).
  • What does commutativity mean for the way you add vectors?
    • The order doesn’t matter.
  • What is a unit vector? How is it represented in this book?
    • Vector of magnitude one that specifies direction. Has no units. A boldface letter with a ^ above it.
  • Go over Example 2.1 - A Ladybug Problem. What is the final position of the ladybug on the meter stick?
    • 68 cm.
  • Where is the geometric approach to adding vectors commonly used?
    • Navigation.
  • What are two methods of adding vectors in two dimensions?
    • The parallelogram rule and head-to-tail construction.
  • Understand Figure 2.10 to understand the parallelogram rule.
  • The tail-to-head geometric construction involves drawing vectors from _______ to _______.
    • Tail to head.
  • Go over Example 2.2 – Geometric Construction of the Resultant. Are A + B and A - B pointing in the same direction?
    • No.

2.2 Coordinate Systems and Components of a Vector

  • What is the x component and the y component of a vector?
    • Projections of a vector onto the x and y axes.
  • What is a unit vector? How are they represented symbolically? What do the i and j correspond to? What is the component form of a vector?
    • Vectors of length one that define orthogonal directions on the plane. Along x – i^, along y – j^. Along x – i^, along y – j^. The magnitude of the x component of a vector is multiplied by i^ and the magnitude of the y component of a vector is multiplied by j^.
