Chapter 13 – Gravitation

Introduction

- Gravity is an action-at-a-distance force, what does this mean? Objects exert a gravitational force on one another without physical contact and that force goes to zero at infinite distance.

- In general, the examples in this chapter are good to see how to put in typical numbers into the relevant equations.

13.1 – Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

- What is the equation for Newton's Law of Gravitation? Equation 13.1.

- What is the driection of the force? Along a line between the centers of m_1 and m_2.

- What is the universal gravitational constant? G=6.67e^(-11)Nm^2/kg^2.

- What is the problem solving strategy for solving problems involving Newton's Law of Gravitation? 1. Identify the two masses, one or both, for which you wish to find the gravitational force. 2. Draw a free-body diagram, sketching the force acting on each mass and indicating the distance between their centers of mass. 3. Apply Newton’s second law of motion to each mass to determine how it will move.

13.5 - Kepler's Laws of Planetary motion

- What is Kepler's first law? Every planet moves along an ellipse, with the Sun at one focus.

- What is Kepler's second law? A planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times. The areal velocity is constant.

- What is Kepler's third law? Equation 13.11.

13.6 - Tidal Forces

- What is a tidal force? Difference between the gravitational force at the center of a body and that at any other location on the body.

- What are spring and neap tides? See Figure 13.22. Spring - When Earth, Moon, and Sun are aligned and the tidal effects add. Neap - When the Earth, Moon and Sun cause the smallest tides.

13.7 Einstein's Theory of Gravity

- What is the principle of equivalence? Part of general relativity that states that there is no difference between free fall and being weightless, or a uniform gravitational field and uniform acceleration.
